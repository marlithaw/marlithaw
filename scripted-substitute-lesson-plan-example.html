Prepositional Phrases Lesson Plan Bundle

Detailed Implementation Guides for Grades 3-5

Overview: This comprehensive lesson plan bundle provides teachers with detailed, minute-by-minute guidance for teaching prepositional phrases across grades 3-5. Each lesson includes specific learning goals, materials lists, timed activities, teacher scripts, anticipated student responses, and assessment guidance within a vertically aligned framework.

Research Foundation: These lessons integrate principles from both the Science of Reading and Science of Writing while implementing the Gradual Release of Responsibility model to scaffold instruction appropriately for diverse learners.

Vertical Alignment: The lessons progress in complexity from grade 3 (identification) to grade 4 (application) to grade 5 (evaluation), with consistent instructional routines and assessment approaches.

GRADE 3 LESSON PLANS

Foundation Level - Focus on "Where" Prepositional Phrases

Lesson 1: Introduction to Prepositional Phrases

Time Required: 45 minutes

Learning Objective: Students will identify prepositional phrases that tell where and explain how they add meaning to sentences.

Standards Alignment:

  • Reading: Identify and understand text features that contribute to meaning
  • Writing: Use descriptive details to enhance writing
  • Language: Understand prepositional phrases in context

Materials Needed:

  • Visual chart with common prepositions (in, on, under, beside, between, etc.)
  • Prepared sentence strips with and without prepositional phrases
  • Large pictures for demonstration (playground, classroom, park)
  • Student workbooks or activity sheets
  • Colored pencils or highlighters (one per student)
  • Location word cards (one set per pair)

Preparation:

  • Create a visual anchor chart displaying common prepositions
  • Prepare sentence strips with and without prepositional phrases
  • Make copies of student activity sheets
  • Arrange classroom for partner work
Time Activity Detailed Description
0-5 minutes Introduction & Engagement

Begin with a movement activity to introduce the concept of location.

Teacher Script: "Today we're going to learn about special words that tell us where things are located. Let's all stand up! When I say a location word, I want you to show me that location with your hands or body."

"Put your hands above your head."

"Place your hand under your chin."

"Stand beside your desk."

"Put your pencil on your paper."

"These words—above, under, beside, on—are called prepositions. Prepositions are words that show location or position."

Expected Student Actions: Students will follow the directions, physically demonstrating each prepositional relationship.
5-15 minutes Explicit Instruction (I Do)

Introduce the concept of prepositional phrases using visual examples.

Teacher Script: "Prepositions are always part of a group of words called a prepositional phrase. A prepositional phrase includes the preposition and the object—the thing or place it's talking about."

*Display playground picture*

"Look at this picture. I'm going to make two sentences about it, and I want you to listen for which one gives more information."

"Sentence 1: The boy plays."

"Sentence 2: The boy plays on the slide."

"Which sentence tells us more information? Yes, the second one. The words 'on the slide' tell us WHERE the boy is playing. 'On the slide' is a prepositional phrase. 'On' is the preposition, and 'the slide' is the object."

*Write the sentence and underline the prepositional phrase*

"Let me show you another example."

*Display classroom picture*

"Sentence 1: The books are."

"Sentence 2: The books are on the shelf."

"Again, which sentence gives us more information? The second one tells us WHERE the books are located. 'On the shelf' is a prepositional phrase. 'On' is the preposition, and 'the shelf' is the object."

Expected Student Responses: Students should identify the second sentence as more informative in each pair. They may begin to recognize that prepositional phrases add location details.
15-25 minutes Guided Practice (We Do)

Work together to identify prepositional phrases in sentences.

Teacher Script: "Now let's practice finding prepositional phrases together. I have some sentence strips here, and we'll read them together to find the prepositional phrases that tell where."

*Display first sentence strip: "The cat sleeps under the bed."*

"Let's read this sentence together." *Class reads sentence aloud*

"Now, what is this sentence about? Yes, it's about a cat sleeping. Where is the cat sleeping? That's right, under the bed. So 'under the bed' is our prepositional phrase. 'Under' is the preposition, and 'the bed' is the object."

*Underline the prepositional phrase on the sentence strip*

*Display second sentence strip: "The children play in the park."*

"Let's read this one together." *Class reads sentence aloud*

"What is this sentence about? Yes, children playing. Now, does this sentence tell us where they are playing? Can anyone identify the prepositional phrase?"

Expected Student Responses: "The prepositional phrase is 'in the park.' 'In' is the preposition, and 'the park' is the object."

Continue with 2-3 more examples, gradually releasing responsibility to students for identifying the phrases.
25-35 minutes Partner Practice (You Do Together)

Students work in pairs to identify prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now you'll work with a partner to find prepositional phrases. I'm giving each pair a set of six sentence cards. Take turns reading the sentences aloud. Then together, highlight or underline the prepositional phrase that tells where. Remember, look for the preposition and then find the object it goes with."

"After you find the prepositional phrase, discuss how it helps you understand where something is happening or located. Would the sentence make sense without this information?"

*Distribute sentence cards to each pair*

"You'll have about 10 minutes to work together. I'll be coming around to listen to your discussions."

Expected Partner Work: Students take turns reading sentences and collaboratively identify prepositional phrases. Sample sentences include:

  • "The turtle hid inside its shell." (inside its shell)
  • "We found shells along the beach." (along the beach)
  • "The bird built a nest on the branch." (on the branch)
  • "The pencil rolled under the table." (under the table)
  • "The fish swims in the aquarium." (in the aquarium)
  • "The dog slept beside the fireplace." (beside the fireplace)
35-42 minutes Independent Practice (You Do Alone)

Students complete a short individual activity identifying prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now it's time to practice on your own. On this activity sheet, you'll find six sentences. For each one:

  1. Underline the prepositional phrase that tells where
  2. Circle just the preposition
  3. Draw a box around the object of the preposition

"Then, for the last two questions, you'll add your own prepositional phrase to complete each sentence. Remember to use a preposition that tells where."

*Distribute activity sheets*

"You'll have about 7 minutes to work on this. If you finish early, try writing your own sentence with a prepositional phrase on the back of your paper."

Activity Sheet Content:

  1. The frog jumped into the pond.
  2. The bird sings on the fence.
  3. The children read books in the library.
  4. The rabbit hopped across the field.
  5. The mouse hid behind the refrigerator.
  6. The teacher wrote notes on the board.
  7. The dog sleeps ________________.
  8. We found the lost toy ________________.
42-45 minutes Closure & Assessment

Review key learning and check for understanding.

Teacher Script: "Let's review what we learned today. Who can tell me what a preposition is?" (Wait for responses)

"That's right. Prepositions are words that show location or position. And when we put a preposition together with an object, we get what's called...?" (Wait for responses)

"Yes, a prepositional phrase! Today we focused on prepositional phrases that tell us where things are located. Tomorrow, we'll practice using these phrases in our writing to make our sentences more interesting and clear."

"Before we finish, let's do a quick check. Everyone put your thumb up if you're confident about identifying prepositional phrases, thumb sideways if you're still a little unsure, and thumb down if you need more help."

*Observe student responses to gauge understanding*

Expected Student Actions: Students indicate their level of understanding with thumbs up/sideways/down, allowing teacher to identify who needs additional support.

Assessment Opportunities:

  • Formative: Monitor partner discussions during the "You Do Together" phase, noting students' ability to correctly identify prepositional phrases.
  • Individual: Collect and review the activity sheets from the "You Do Alone" phase to assess individual understanding.
  • Exit Slip: The thumbs up/sideways/down check provides immediate feedback on student confidence levels.

Success Criteria:

  • Students can correctly identify prepositional phrases in sentences.
  • Students understand that prepositional phrases add location information to sentences.
  • Students can distinguish between the preposition and its object within a phrase.
  • Students can create simple prepositional phrases to complete sentences.

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: For students who need additional help, provide a word bank of common prepositions for reference. Consider using color-coding to visually distinguish prepositions (one color) from objects (another color).
  • Extension: Challenge advanced students to write original sentences with multiple prepositional phrases or to identify prepositional phrases in a short paragraph.
  • ELL Support: Provide visual cues showing the spatial relationships indicated by each preposition. Allow ELL students to draw pictures showing the prepositional relationships before writing.

Lesson 2: Visualizing with Prepositional Phrases

Time Required: 45 minutes

Learning Objective: Students will identify how prepositional phrases help readers visualize location and use prepositional phrases in their own writing to create clear mental images.

Standards Alignment:

  • Reading: Use visualization strategies to enhance comprehension
  • Writing: Use descriptive details to create mental images
  • Language: Apply knowledge of prepositional phrases in writing

Materials Needed:

  • Sentence pairs (with/without prepositional phrases) on chart paper
  • Drawing paper (one per student)
  • Colored pencils or crayons
  • Prepositional phrase picture cards
  • Preposition word bank poster
  • Student writing journals
  • Visualization activity sheets

Preparation:

  • Write sentence pairs on chart paper
  • Prepare visualization activity sheets
  • Set up drawing station with supplies
  • Display preposition word bank from previous lesson
Time Activity Detailed Description
0-5 minutes Review & Engagement

Quickly review prior learning and engage students with a visualization activity.

Teacher Script: "Yesterday we learned about prepositional phrases that tell where. Who can remind us what a preposition is? And what is a prepositional phrase?" (Call on volunteers)

"Great! Today we're going to explore how prepositional phrases help us create pictures in our minds when we read. Let's start with a quick activity. I want everyone to close their eyes."

"I'm going to say two sentences. As I say each one, try to create a picture in your mind."

"Sentence 1: The cat sat."

*Pause for 3 seconds*

"Sentence 2: The cat sat on the windowsill in the kitchen."

*Pause for 3 seconds*

"Open your eyes. Raise your hand if you had a clearer picture in your mind with the second sentence. Why do you think that is?"

Expected Student Responses: Students should recognize that the second sentence created a more specific mental image because it included location details. They might say things like, "I could see where the cat was sitting" or "I could picture the kitchen and the window."
5-15 minutes Explicit Instruction (I Do)

Demonstrate how prepositional phrases create clear mental images.

Teacher Script: "When we read, our brains try to create pictures of what's happening. Prepositional phrases help us make those pictures clearer because they tell us where things are located."

*Display first sentence pair on chart paper*

"Let's look at these sentences:"

"A: The children played."

"B: The children played in the sprinkler on the front lawn."

"I'm going to think aloud about the pictures these sentences create in my mind."

"For sentence A, I can see children playing, but I don't know where they are or what they're playing with. It's a very general picture."

"For sentence B, I can see children running through a sprinkler. I can see water spraying. I know they're on a lawn in front of a house. The prepositional phrases 'in the sprinkler' and 'on the front lawn' help me create this specific picture."

*Underline the prepositional phrases*

"Let me show you how I would draw these two different sentences."

*Quickly sketch simple drawings representing each sentence*

"See how my second drawing has specific details about location? That's the power of prepositional phrases—they help readers visualize exactly where things are happening."

Expected Student Actions: Students observe the differences between the two drawings and notice how the prepositional phrases created a more detailed visualization.
15-25 minutes Guided Practice (We Do)

Work together to visualize and draw scenes based on prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now let's practice visualizing with prepositional phrases together. I'm going to show you another sentence pair."

*Display second sentence pair*

"A: The bird built a nest."

"B: The bird built a nest between the branches at the top of the oak tree."

"Let's read these together." *Class reads sentences aloud*

"Now, I'd like us to draw what we see for sentence B. What prepositional phrases help us visualize the location of the nest?"

*Wait for student responses*

"That's right! 'Between the branches' and 'at the top of the oak tree' are our prepositional phrases. Let's underline them."

*Underline phrases on chart paper*

"Now, let's draw this together. Based on these prepositional phrases, where should we draw the nest?"

Expected Student Responses: Students should identify that the nest needs to be drawn between branches and at the top of an oak tree. They may suggest specific visualization details based on the prepositional phrases.

Guide students through a collaborative drawing based on the prepositional phrases, asking questions to ensure they're using the location details correctly.
25-35 minutes Partner Practice (You Do Together)

Students work in pairs to visualize and draw based on sentences with prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now you'll work with a partner on a visualization challenge. Each pair will get an envelope with three sentences inside. Each sentence contains prepositional phrases that tell where."

"For each sentence:"

  1. Read the sentence together
  2. Underline the prepositional phrases
  3. Discuss what you visualize based on these phrases
  4. Work together to draw what the sentence describes, making sure to show the locations exactly as described by the prepositional phrases

*Distribute envelopes with sentence strips*

"You'll have about 10 minutes to work with your partner. Remember to use the prepositional phrases to guide your drawing."

Sample Sentences for Partner Work:

  • "The puppy hid under the bed behind the toy box."
  • "The butterfly landed on the flower in the middle of the garden."
  • "The treasure was buried beneath the large rock near the old oak tree."

Expected Partner Actions: Partners should discuss the visualization, identify the prepositional phrases, and create drawings that accurately represent the locations described in the phrases.
35-42 minutes Independent Practice (You Do Alone)

Students create their own sentences with prepositional phrases and illustrate them.

Teacher Script: "Now it's your turn to create a sentence with prepositional phrases and illustrate it. On your activity sheet, you'll see a simple sentence starter. Your job is to:"

  1. Add at least two prepositional phrases that tell where
  2. Draw a picture showing exactly what your sentence describes
  3. Underline the prepositional phrases in your sentence

"Use our preposition word bank to help you if needed."

*Point to word bank poster and distribute activity sheets*

"You'll have about 7 minutes to complete this. Make sure your drawing matches exactly what your prepositional phrases describe!"

Activity Sheet Content:

Sentence starters:

  • "The squirrel jumped ___________________ ___________________."
  • "The boy found his missing toy ___________________ ___________________."
  • "My favorite place to read is ___________________ ___________________."

(Students choose one starter to complete)

Expected Student Work: Students should add appropriate prepositional phrases and create illustrations that accurately depict the locations described.
42-45 minutes Closure & Assessment

Summarize learning and check for understanding.

Teacher Script: "Today we learned how prepositional phrases help us visualize where things are happening. Let's share a few of your sentences and drawings."

*Call on 2-3 volunteers to share their completed sentences and illustrations*

"Excellent work! Now, let's finish with a quick reflection. On the back of your activity sheet, complete this sentence:"

"Prepositional phrases help readers visualize by _______________________."

*Allow 1 minute for students to write*

"Tomorrow, we'll continue our work with prepositional phrases by using them to make our own writing more interesting and detailed."

Expected Student Reflections: Students might write responses such as "Prepositional phrases help readers visualize by showing exactly where things are located" or "Prepositional phrases help readers visualize by giving specific details about position."

Assessment Opportunities:

  • Formative: Monitor partner discussions and drawings to assess understanding of how prepositional phrases create visualization.
  • Individual: Review student-created sentences and illustrations to check for appropriate use of prepositional phrases and alignment between text and drawing.
  • Exit Ticket: The written reflection provides insight into student understanding of the purpose of prepositional phrases.

Success Criteria:

  • Students can identify how prepositional phrases enhance visualization.
  • Students create drawings that accurately reflect the locations described by prepositional phrases.
  • Students use prepositional phrases in their own writing to specify location.
  • Students can explain the purpose of prepositional phrases in creating visual imagery.

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: For struggling students, limit to one prepositional phrase at a time and provide sentence frames with the preposition already included (e.g., "The squirrel jumped from _____.").
  • Extension: Challenge advanced students to use three or more prepositional phrases in a single sentence and to consider how the order of phrases affects visualization.
  • ELL Support: Pair ELL students with language models, provide illustrated preposition cards, and allow drawing before writing to establish concept understanding.

Lesson 3: Using Prepositional Phrases in Writing

Time Required: 45 minutes

Learning Objective: Students will enhance their writing by adding prepositional phrases that provide specific location details.

Standards Alignment:

  • Writing: Develop writing by adding specific details
  • Language: Apply knowledge of prepositional phrases in original writing
  • Reading: Recognize how authors use prepositional phrases for effect

Materials Needed:

  • Mentor text with highlighted prepositional phrases
  • Chart paper with "Basic Sentences" vs. "Detailed Sentences" columns
  • Sentence revision activity sheets
  • Student writing journals
  • Preposition word bank reference sheets (one per student)
  • Green colored pencils for highlighting (one per student)
  • Descriptive paragraph planning template

Preparation:

  • Select and prepare mentor text with prepositional phrases highlighted
  • Create chart with basic vs. detailed sentences
  • Prepare sentence revision activity sheets
  • Make copies of preposition word bank reference sheets
  • Make copies of paragraph planning templates
Time Activity Detailed Description
0-5 minutes Review & Engagement

Review previous learning and introduce the writing focus.

Teacher Script: "Over the past two days, we've learned about prepositional phrases that tell where. We've identified them in sentences and seen how they help readers visualize location. Who can remind us what a prepositional phrase is?" (Call on volunteers)

"Excellent! Today, we're going to use what we've learned as writers. Authors use prepositional phrases to make their writing more interesting and to help readers picture exactly where things are happening. Let's look at how a real author does this."

Expected Student Responses: Students should recall that prepositional phrases include a preposition and an object, and they tell where something is located.
5-15 minutes Explicit Instruction (I Do)

Model how to enhance writing by adding prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "I'm going to read a short paragraph from our mentor text. Listen for the prepositional phrases that tell where."

*Read selected paragraph, emphasizing the prepositional phrases*

"Did you notice how the author used prepositional phrases to describe exactly where things were happening? Let's look at these highlighted phrases."

*Point out highlighted prepositional phrases in the text*

"Now I'm going to show you how we can improve our own writing by adding prepositional phrases. Watch as I revise this basic paragraph."

*Display chart with basic paragraph*

Basic paragraph: "The boy sat. He ate lunch. Birds flew. People walked. Music played."

"This paragraph doesn't give us much information about where anything is happening. I'm going to think aloud as I revise it by adding prepositional phrases."

"The boy sat... Where did he sit? How about: The boy sat on the park bench."

"He ate lunch... Where? Maybe: He ate lunch under the shady oak tree."

"Birds flew... Let's add: Birds flew above the pond."

"People walked... Where? How about: People walked along the winding path."

"Music played... Where was it coming from? Maybe: Music played from a radio near the playground."

*Write the revised version on chart paper as you think aloud*

"Let me read the revised paragraph: 'The boy sat on the park bench. He ate lunch under the shady oak tree. Birds flew above the pond. People walked along the winding path. Music played from a radio near the playground.'"

"Do you see how adding prepositional phrases makes the writing more interesting and helps readers visualize the scene better? The prepositional phrases create a clear picture of the park setting."

Expected Student Actions: Students observe how adding prepositional phrases transforms basic sentences into more vivid, detailed descriptions.
15-25 minutes Guided Practice (We Do)

Work together to enhance a paragraph by adding prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now let's practice revising together. Here's another simple paragraph:"

*Display on chart paper*

"The cat slept. The children played. A dog barked. Leaves fell. The teacher read."

"Let's revise this paragraph by adding prepositional phrases that tell where. For each sentence, I'll ask: 'Where did this happen?' Then we'll add an appropriate prepositional phrase."

"Let's start with the first sentence: 'The cat slept.' Where might the cat be sleeping?"

*Call on students for suggestions*

"Great suggestions! Let's use: 'The cat slept on the windowsill.'"

*Write the revised sentence on chart paper*

"Now for the next sentence: 'The children played.' Where were the children playing?"

Expected Student Suggestions:

  • "The children played in the sandbox."
  • "The children played on the jungle gym."
  • "The children played behind the school."

*Teacher selects one suggestion and continues through the remaining sentences with student input*

Continue the process for each sentence, guiding students to create a cohesive paragraph where the prepositional phrases create a consistent setting.
25-35 minutes Partner Practice (You Do Together)

Students work in pairs to revise a paragraph by adding prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now you'll work with a partner to revise a paragraph by adding prepositional phrases. Each pair will get a worksheet with a simple paragraph about a classroom. Your job is to:"

  1. Read the basic paragraph together
  2. Discuss where each action might be happening
  3. Add prepositional phrases to each sentence to show location
  4. Make sure your prepositional phrases create a clear picture of the classroom

*Distribute activity sheets and preposition word bank reference sheets*

"You'll have about 10 minutes to work together. Remember, good writers use prepositional phrases to help readers visualize the setting."

Basic Paragraph for Revision:

"Students worked. Pencils moved. Books lay. The clock ticked. The teacher smiled."

Sample Revised Paragraph:

"Students worked at their desks. Pencils moved across notebook pages. Books lay on the colorful rug. The clock ticked above the whiteboard. The teacher smiled behind her desk."
35-42 minutes Independent Practice (You Do Alone)

Students write a descriptive paragraph using prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Now you'll have a chance to write your own descriptive paragraph. I'd like you to write a paragraph describing a place—it could be your bedroom, the cafeteria, a park, or anywhere you can picture clearly."

"First, use the planning template to list the items or actions you want to include. Then, write a complete paragraph with at least five sentences. Each sentence should include at least one prepositional phrase that tells where."

"Remember to use different prepositions to make your writing interesting. Use your preposition word bank if you need ideas."

*Distribute paragraph planning templates*

"You'll have about 7 minutes to plan and write your paragraph. When you finish, underline each prepositional phrase you used."

Planning Template Structure:

Place I am describing: ______________________

Things/people in this place:

  1. ________________
  2. ________________
  3. ________________
  4. ________________
  5. ________________

Prepositions I will use:

  1. ________________
  2. ________________
  3. ________________
  4. ________________
  5. ________________

My paragraph:

(Space for writing)
42-45 minutes Closure & Assessment

Share student work and reflect on learning.

Teacher Script: "Let's share some of your wonderful descriptive paragraphs. Who would like to read their paragraph aloud? As we listen, we'll pay attention to the prepositional phrases and how they help us visualize the setting."

*Call on 2-3 volunteers to share their paragraphs*

"Wonderful writing! I noticed how you all used prepositional phrases to create clear pictures of your settings."

"Let's end with a quick reflection. On the bottom of your planning sheet, complete this sentence:"

"Adding prepositional phrases to my writing helps my readers by _______________________."

*Allow 1 minute for students to write*

"Tomorrow, we'll continue exploring how prepositional phrases can make our writing more precise and interesting."

Expected Student Reflections: Students might write responses such as "Adding prepositional phrases to my writing helps my readers by showing them exactly where things are located" or "Adding prepositional phrases to my writing helps my readers by creating clearer pictures in their minds."

Assessment Opportunities:

  • Formative: Monitor partner discussions during the paragraph revision activity to assess understanding of how prepositional phrases enhance writing.
  • Individual: Collect and review students' original paragraphs to evaluate their ability to incorporate prepositional phrases effectively in their writing.
  • Reflection: Review student reflections to gauge their understanding of the purpose of prepositional phrases in writing.

Success Criteria:

  • Students add appropriate prepositional phrases to basic sentences to enhance meaning.
  • Students use a variety of prepositions in their writing.
  • Students create a cohesive paragraph where prepositional phrases establish a clear setting.
  • Students can explain how prepositional phrases improve their writing.

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: Provide sentence frames with blanks specifically for prepositional phrases. Offer a more structured planning template with prepositions already suggested.
  • Extension: Challenge advanced students to write a paragraph comparing two different settings, using prepositional phrases to highlight the contrasts. Encourage them to experiment with prepositional phrases at different positions in sentences.
  • ELL Support: Pre-teach key vocabulary for the setting students will describe. Allow drawing before writing. Provide model sentences with prepositional phrases in students' home language and English.

GRADE 4 LESSON PLANS

Application Level - Focus on "Where," "When," and "How" Prepositional Phrases

Lesson 1: Types of Prepositional Phrases

Time Required: 45 minutes

Learning Objective: Students will identify and categorize prepositional phrases by function (where, when, how) and explain how they add specific information to sentences.

Standards Alignment:

  • Reading: Analyze how specific language contributes to meaning
  • Writing: Use specific language to convey experiences and events
  • Language: Analyze the function of prepositional phrases in context

Materials Needed:

  • Function-color coded anchor chart (where = blue, when = orange, how = green)
  • Mentor text with highlighted prepositional phrases
  • Sentence strips with different types of prepositional phrases
  • Function sorting cards (where, when, how headers)
  • Individual recording sheets for categorization activity
  • Colored highlighters or markers (blue, orange, green) for each student
  • Preposition function reference guide

Preparation:

  • Create function-color coded anchor chart
  • Prepare sentence strips with varied prepositional phrase types
  • Make copies of reference guides and recording sheets
  • Set up sorting stations for partner work
Time Activity Detailed Description
0-5 minutes Review & Engagement

Activate prior knowledge and introduce the focus on prepositional phrase functions.

Teacher Script: "In third grade, you learned about prepositional phrases that tell where. Who can remind us what a prepositional phrase is?" (Call on volunteers)

"Excellent! A prepositional phrase includes a preposition and an object. Last year, you focused on prepositional phrases that tell where things are located. Today, we're going to discover that prepositional phrases can also tell when something happens and how something is done."

"Let's look at a sentence: 'During the storm, the family played games with great enthusiasm.'"

"This sentence has two prepositional phrases, but they give us different types of information. Let's investigate what each one tells us."

Expected Student Responses: Students should recall that prepositional phrases include a preposition and an object, and that they've previously learned about phrases that show location.
5-15 minutes Explicit Instruction (I Do)

Introduce and model the three functions of prepositional phrases.

Teacher Script: "Prepositional phrases can serve different functions in a sentence. They can tell us where, when, or how. Let me show you how to identify each type."

*Display function-color coded anchor chart*

"We'll use different colors to help us remember each function: blue for where, orange for when, and green for how."

"Let's go back to our sentence: 'During the storm, the family played games with great enthusiasm.'"

"To identify the function of a prepositional phrase, I ask myself what question it answers:"

  • Where? (location) → Blue
  • When? (time) → Orange
  • How? (manner) → Green

"Let me think about 'during the storm.' This tells us when the family played games. So I'll highlight this in orange because it tells when."

*Highlight 'during the storm' in orange*

"Now let's look at 'with great enthusiasm.' Does this tell us where? No. Does it tell us when? No. Does it tell us how the family played games? Yes! It describes the manner in which they played. So I'll highlight this in green because it tells how."

*Highlight 'with great enthusiasm' in green*

"Let me show you a few more examples."

*Display sentence: "The children raced across the field before sunset."*

"'Across the field' tells where, so I'll highlight it in blue. 'Before sunset' tells when, so I'll highlight it in orange."

*Display sentence: "The chef prepared the meal with careful precision in the restaurant kitchen."*

"'With careful precision' tells how, so I'll highlight it in green. 'In the restaurant kitchen' tells where, so I'll highlight it in blue."

Expected Student Actions: Students observe the teacher's modeling of how to identify the different functions of prepositional phrases using the questioning strategy and color-coding system.
15-25 minutes Guided Practice (We Do)

Practice identifying and categorizing prepositional phrases together.

Teacher Script: "Now let's practice identifying the functions of prepositional phrases together. I'll display sentences, and we'll work through them step by step."

*Display sentence: "After dinner, the family walked through the park with their dog."*

"First, let's identify all the prepositional phrases in this sentence." (Wait for student responses)

"Great! We have 'after dinner,' 'through the park,' and 'with their dog.' Now let's determine the function of each phrase by asking our questions."

"'After dinner' - Does this tell us where, when, or how? Let's think about it."

Expected Student Responses:

"'After dinner' tells when, so it should be orange."

"'Through the park' tells where, so it should be blue."

"'With their dog' tells how or with whom, so it should be green."

Continue with 2-3 more sentences, gradually increasing student participation in identifying functions:

*Display sentence: "The students worked in groups during class with great focus."*

"Who can identify a prepositional phrase in this sentence and tell us its function?" (Call on volunteers)

*Display sentence: "Before the bell, the teacher organized papers on her desk with care."*

"Who else would like to identify a prepositional phrase and its function?"
25-35 minutes Partner Practice (You Do Together)

Students work in pairs to sort and categorize prepositional phrases by function.

Teacher Script: "Now you'll work with a partner on a sorting activity. Each pair will receive an envelope with sentence strips. Each sentence contains at least one prepositional phrase."

"Your job is to:"

  1. Read each sentence carefully
